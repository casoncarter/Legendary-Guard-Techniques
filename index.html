Dribbling Mechanics & Ball Control

Build a guard handle that works against real pressure: float dribbles to change pace, hesitations to freeze defenders, Nash-style probes in the paint, and escape techniques for traps like Trae Young.

1. Float Dribble (Change of Pace)

  • Start in a relaxed stance, ball in your strong hand.
  • Push the ball out slightly in front of you, then “hang” it with a soft, controlled dribble.
  • Let your body rise up (almost like you’re standing taller) to sell that you might pull up or stop.
  • As the defender relaxes or stands up, explode past with a long, low push step.
  • Work both hands, and practice using the float into pull-ups, drives, and step-backs.
Float Dribble ▶
The Float Dribble: Why Every Player Needs This Use this to change pace, keep defenders guessing, and set up your next move.

2. Hesitation Dribble (Jamal Murray)

  • Attack downhill, then let the ball “hang” at your side as you slightly rise up.
  • Eyes up at the rim as if you might shoot or pull up to force the defender to react.
  • As soon as the defender relaxes or shifts, drop your hips and explode past with one hard dribble.
  • Mix in hesitations into step-back threes, pull-ups, and drives to both hands.
Jamal Murray ▶
Jamal Murray Basketball Moves For Guards Study his hang dribble, hesitation timing, and change of speed out of the pick-and-roll.

3. Nash Dribble (Paint Probe)

  • Use a tight, controlled dribble to circle under the basket without picking the ball up.
  • Keep your eyes scanning: corner shooters, dunker spot, roller, and weak-side help.
  • If nothing is open, keep dribbling out the other side and re-attack—do not force a shot.
  • This move turns the paint into your “office” where you read the defense while still keeping your dribble.
Steve Nash ▶
Master the Nash Dribble | Become a Paint Playmaker Learn how to probe with the dribble and create layups, kick-outs, and dump-offs.

4. Escaping Traps (Trae Young)

  • When a trap comes, do not pick up your dribble unless you have a clear pass.
  • Use retreat dribbles (backward dribble) to pull the traps away from the basket.
  • Turn your body to shield the ball, using your outside leg and hip as protection.
  • See over the trap to find the open teammate—usually one pass away or diagonally opposite.

Handle Labs

Float · Hesitation · Nash Probe · Trap Escape

Use these to build real game pressure handling: changing pace, probing the paint, and escaping traps without picking up your dribble.

Drill 1 · Float Dribble Lane (Both Sides)

Handle · Change of Pace ▶
The Float Dribble: Why Every Player Needs This Learn how to hang the ball, rise up, and then explode past each defender or cone.
  • Place 4 cones down each lane line, 8–10 feet apart.
  • Start at the top, attack down the right lane using a float (hang) dribble at each cone.
  • Rise slightly to sell “stop or pull-up,” then punch out low and long past the cone.
  • Finish with a layup or floater, then repeat down the left lane with your weak hand.

Drill 2 · Hesitation into Burst (Wing)

Handle · Hesi ▶
Jamal Murray Basketball Moves For Guards Study the hang hesitation, eyes to the rim, and the burst once the defender stands up.
  • Start at the top, attack the wing cone at game speed.
  • At the cone, let the ball “hang” and rise up like you might shoot.
  • As soon as the defender relaxes or shifts, drop your hips and explode past.
  • Finish with a pull-up, floater, or layup off either foot and both hands.

Drill 3 · Nash Dribble Paint Laps

Handle · Paint Probe ▶
Master the Nash Dribble | Become a Paint Playmaker Learn to keep your dribble alive under the rim and read corners, rollers, and skips.
  • Drive into the paint, keep your dribble alive, and circle under the basket.
  • On each lap, call out your read: “corner,” “roller,” or “skip.”
  • Stay low, use your body to shield the ball, and keep your eyes up the whole time.
  • Exit on strong-side corner, weak-side corner, or re-attack the paint.

Drill 4 · Trap Escape Box (Trae Young)

Handle · vs Traps ▶
Trae Young’s Dribbling Secrets vs Traps Watch how he retreats, changes angles, and finds open teammates out of doubles.
  • Set up a small cone “box” at halfcourt with 2 cones/chairs as the trap in front.
  • Start inside the box, face the trap, and work on retreat dribbles and direction changes.
  • Never pick up your dribble until you have a clear passing window.
  • When you escape, call out and “pass” to the open spot: “middle,” “opposite,” or “short.”
Shooting Labs

Steph Curry Shooting Blueprint

All shooting work here is Steph-inspired: balance, quick but relaxed release, deep range, and constant movement without the ball. Start close, then build out to game threes and relocations.

Shooting Drill 1 · Steph Curry Form Ladder

Shooting · Form ▶
7 Keys to Shoot Like Steph Curry Use this as the model for your base: feet, balance, one-motion release, and arc.
  • Start 3–5 feet from the hoop; make 5 “perfect” swishes from each close spot.
  • Copy Steph’s base: wide balance, relaxed shoulders, smooth one-motion release.
  • After you make your number from a spot, take one step back and repeat.
  • Count total makes out of 50–100 shots and track how your “Steph base” improves.

Shooting Drill 2 · Curry Game-Spot Shooting

Shooting · Game Spots ▶
3 Basketball Shooting Drills For Guards Hit the same slots Steph lives in: corners, wings, and top, off catch and 1–2 dribbles.
  • Choose 5–7 Curry-style spots: corners, wings, top, and the “Curry slot.”
  • From each spot: 5–10 catch-and-shoot threes, then 5–10 1–2 dribble pull-ups.
  • Prep like Steph: hands up early, feet set as the ball arrives, quick but under-control release.
  • Track makes at each spot and try to beat your numbers every week.

Shooting Drill 3 · Curry Relocation & Off-Movement

Shooting · Movement ▶
Train Like Steph – Shooting Off the Move Pass, relocate, and sprint into shots the way Steph hunts space after giving it up.
  • Start at the slot, pass to a partner (or self-toss), then sprint to a new spot (corner, wing, slot).
  • Curl or fade into the catch, square in the air, and land balanced like Steph.
  • Go 3–5 minute blocks where you never stand still: pass, relocate, shoot, repeat.
  • Emphasize conditioning and shot quality: game speed cuts + Steph-quality mechanics.
Defense Labs

Gary Payton & Wojo Defensive Blueprint

These drills build a Gary Payton / Wojo mindset: nasty on-ball pressure, relentless slides, and disciplined closeouts. Treat defense as your superpower, not an afterthought.

Defense Drill 1 · Payton / Wojo Stance & Slides

Defense · Stance ▶
Defensive Stance and Slides Build the low, aggressive base Payton and Wojo played with every possession.
  • Start on the baseline in a Payton-level stance: chest up, hips low, hands active.
  • Slide to the free throw line extended, staying low and never clicking your feet together.
  • Slide back; that’s 1 rep. Do 4–6 reps, rest, then 3–4 total sets.
  • Channel Wojo: quiet feet, loud voice (“ball, ball, ball”), and elite focus.

Defense Drill 2 · Payton Zig-Zag Cut-Offs

Defense · Lateral Quickness ▶
Zig Zag Defensive Slides Warmup Train like a point-of-attack pest who beats the ball to every spot.
  • Set cones up the sideline in a zig-zag pattern (every 8–10 feet).
  • From baseline to halfcourt, defensive slide cone to cone, opening your hips to cut off drives.
  • Imagine you’re Payton: your job is to turn the ball handler 2–3 times before halfcourt.
  • Go up and back 3–5 times as a warm-up or finisher; stay low the entire time.

Defense Drill 3 · Wojo Closeout Series

Defense · Closeouts ▶
“2 Minute Closeouts” – Daily Defensive Drill Short, intense reps to build Wojo-style closeout footwork and hand discipline.
  • Start under the rim; coach or partner calls out “corner,” “wing,” or “top.”
  • Sprint halfway to the shooter, then chop your feet into a balanced closeout.
  • Arrive under control with high hands: take away the shot without flying by or fouling.
  • Go in 2-minute blocks with as many locked-in, Wojo-quality reps as possible.

Coach K Leadership Lab

This section is about approach, attitude, and leadership. Use it as your mental training block at the end of each day.

Mindset & Standards

“Standards, Not Just Rules”

Coach K’s teams were built on shared standards: how you talk, how you compete, and how you treat teammates. As a guard, you set the tone.

Next Play Mentality
Communicate Constantly
Selfless Competitiveness
Arrive Early, Stay Late

Daily Leadership Checklist

  • Next Play – After every mistake or big play, reset your body language within 3 seconds.
  • Communication – Call out screens, matchups, and coverages every possession you’re on the floor.
  • Selfless Energy – Celebrate teammates’ success louder than your own.
  • Preparation = Confidence – Know your workout block before you step on the court.
  • Standards Talk – If a teammate’s effort slips, talk to them privately and directly.

Weekly Reflection Prompts

  • When did I live our team standards this week?
  • Where did my body language slip?
  • Who did I encourage? Who can I encourage next week?
  • What did I prepare well for? What did I wing?

“Leadership is not about you; it’s about the people you serve. The moment you understand that, you start to lead the way your team needs you to.”

— Inspired by Coach K’s leadership philosophy

Use your guard spot to:

  • Organize huddles before free throws and after timeouts.
  • Call mini “reset” huddles when momentum swings either way.
  • Match your voice with your effort and defense.
